# Chapter 21 Knowledge Engineering: Ontologies, Extraction, and Knowledge Graphs

---

This chapter discusses ontologies, extraction, and knowledge graphs in knowledge engineering. It explains how conceptual models, entity relationships, GraphRAG, and knowledge asset governance complement vector retrieval. Vector retrieval is effective for finding similar snippets, but it struggles with questions that require relational reasoning, such as "Which contracts are associated with this supplier, and which contracts trigger which risks?" This chapter clarifies how ontologies model business concepts, how extraction and entity linking turn text into structured facts, and how GraphRAG combines graph paths with textual evidence.

RAG excels at finding evidence within documents, but many enterprise questions ask how entities, relationships, rules, and business definitions fit together. Which group does a client belong to? Which products are constrained by a contract? Which fields do metrics depend on? Which services are affected by an incident? What is the relationship between suppliers and defective batches? These questions require knowledge engineering: distilling business entities, relationships, constraints, and evidence into governable knowledge assets.

## 21.1 Positioning enterprise knowledge engineering

Knowledge engineering is an engineering approach for representing business semantics explicitly. Ontologies define objects and relationships; extraction processes generate facts from documents and systems; entity linking aligns aliases and duplicate objects; graph databases support relational queries; GraphRAG combines graph structures with textual evidence for LLM use. When discussing knowledge engineering, it is important to clearly separate RAG, semantic layers, knowledge graphs, and GraphRAG as shown in Table 21-1, so that all "knowledge enhancement" work does not collapse into one vague solution. Each capability handles different objects and answers different kinds of questions.

*Table 21-1: Boundaries of RAG, Semantic Layers, and Knowledge Graphs. Source: Compiled from this book.*

| Capability           | Primary Objects                         | Good At                                     | Not Good At                         |
|---------------------|----------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Document RAG        | Chunks, documents, citations           | "What does the policy say?""Where is the contract clause?" | Complex relationships, global aggregation, entity disambiguation |
| DataAgent Semantic Layer | Metrics, dimensions, table fields, SQL | "How is this metric calculated?""Which tables to query?" | Unstructured relationships and open-text evidence |
| Knowledge Graph     | Entities, relationships, events, rules | "Which objects affect each other?""What is the relation chain?" | Open generation without evidence text |
| GraphRAG            | Graph structure + textual evidence     | "Cross-document, cross-entity comprehensive answers" | Poor ontology and extraction quality amplifies errors |

These capability boundaries determine the order of platform investment: basic policy Q&A may not require a graph; DataAgent metrics usually enter the semantic layer first; only when questions start to rely on entity relations, impact chains, and cross-document synthesis does the knowledge graph become a core asset. Table 21-2 further breaks this down into investment, maintenance, and security concerns for platform leaders.

Be cautious not to push all knowledge problems onto graphs. Graphs suit stable entities, well-defined relationships, and questions requiring impact tracking; if business objects lack unified keys, document sources frequently change, and relationship definitions vary by team, premature graph building only adds maintenance burden. Many policy Q&A needs are met by good parsing, chunking, citation, and access controls; many metric questions fit better in semantic layers and data catalogs. The criteria for knowledge engineering are not technological sophistication but whether relation modeling reduces errors, improves review efficiency, and gains long-term business owner buy-in.

*Table 21-2: Key Decision Points for Platform Leaders in Knowledge Engineering. Source: Compiled from this book.*

| Decision Question          | Recommended Judgment                                                           |
|---------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Whether to build a knowledge graph now | Worth it if questions involve entity relations, impact analysis, cross-document synthesis, and long-term governance; otherwise start with RAG for general policy Q&A. |
| Whether to deploy GraphRAG | Only after graph, ontology, extraction quality, and evidence chain are stable; errors in graphs become more convincingly wrong in GraphRAG. |
| Who maintains the ontology | Must involve both business owner and platform owner; cannot rely solely on algorithms or single application teams. |
| Where is the security boundary | Nodes, edges, evidence, and community summaries all require permissions; ACLs only on documents are insufficient. |
| Minimum launch threshold    | Each fact must have source, evidence, confidence, extractor version, review status, and lifecycle. |

## 21.2 Ontology modeling and business semantic layer

An ontology is the backbone of knowledge engineering. It defines which entities, relationships, attributes, and constraints the enterprise cares about. Without an ontology, LLM extraction ends up producing "a different set of fields for each batch of documents"; without a business semantic layer, knowledge graphs become isolated collections of nodes that cannot serve DataAgent, RAG, or process automation. The first version of the ontology does not aim to cover all business areas at once, but it must ensure that every entity, relationship, attribute, constraint, and piece of evidence listed in Table 21-3 has maintainable fields.

*Table 21-3: Minimal Enterprise Ontology Objects. Source: Compiled by the authors.*

| Object          | Example                                     | Key Fields                           |
|-----------------|---------------------------------------------|------------------------------------|
| Entity Type     | Customer, Contract, Product, Metric, Service, Work Order, Risk Event | Name, Alias, Business Key, Source System |
| Relation Type   | Signed, Belongs to, Depends on, Affects, Violates, Similar | Direction, Cardinality, Confidence, Evidence Source |
| Attribute       | Contract Amount, Customer Level, Service Owner | Data Type, Unit, Validity Period   |
| Constraint      | A contract must belong to one customer      | Validation Rule, Exception Handling |
| Evidence        | Document snippet, SQL, System log, Manual annotation | Source, Page, Span, Timestamp      |

Together, these objects answer one question: Why is the fact in the graph trustworthy? If there are only entities and relations, but no evidence, constraints, or provenance, GraphRAG simply replaces unverifiable text with an unverifiable graph. Ontology modeling must be driven by business problems, not by graph database syntax. Legal cares about contract clauses, risk types, and approval opinions; operations care about services, dependencies, incidents, and changes; DataAgent cares about metrics, fields, tables, and definitions. Each ontology object must be able to answer: Who maintains it, where does it come from, which problems is it used for, and what impact do errors cause?

The knowledge graph needed by DataAgent may not be large initially, but it must link key business objects: which fields metrics depend on, which tables those fields belong to, which data domains tables come from, which reports and business processes use the metrics, and which historical SQL queries are affected by definition changes. This enables DataAgent to perform impact analysis and explain definitions before generating queries, instead of relying solely on prompts to remember business semantics.

Ontology change management must be as rigorous as code or data model changes. Adding a new entity type may require updating extractors, graph database schema, access control rules, evaluation samples, and downstream tools. Changing the direction of a relationship can alter the meaning of existing path queries. Merging two concepts can affect historical facts and references. A prudent approach is to establish versioning, review, migration, and deprecation mechanisms for the ontology. Business owners are responsible for semantic correctness; platform owners are responsible for interfaces and governance; data owners are responsible for source systems and key consistency. Without this process, the ontology quickly becomes a shared configuration that no one dares to change. The ontology modeling roadmap in Table 21-4 also follows the preceding principle of business-driven design: cold start can be document-driven, but long-term governance must return to business objects and relationship boundaries.

*Table 21-4: Ontology Modeling Trade-offs. Source: Compiled by the authors.*

| Approach                | Advantages                        | Cost                              | Applicable Scenarios        | mini-platform Choice        |
|-------------------------|---------------------------------|----------------------------------|----------------------------|-----------------------------|
| Document-Driven Extraction | Fast startup, covers historical documents | Ontology drifts easily, weak fact consistency | Early exploration, knowledge base enhancement | Used as cold start input     |
| Business-Object Driven Ontology | Stable structure, easier governance and access control | Requires business experts early on | Core assets: contracts, customers, metrics, services | Default approach            |
| Relationship-First Modeling | Suitable for dependency and impact analysis | May ignore attributes and evidence | Operations, supply chain, risk propagation | Used as scenario extension   |
| OWL/RDF Standard Modeling | Rigorous semantic expression, complete standard ecosystem | Higher learning and engineering cost | Compliance, cross-organization data exchange | Investigate first, not default |

For a mini-platform, the conclusion is clear: do not start with a full OWL/RDF system. Instead, first build solid business entities, relations, evidence, and version governance, then introduce standard semantic technologies as needed for compliance or cross-organization exchange. The collaborative ontology modeling method in Figure 21-3 should also follow this principle. The schema should not be designed behind closed doors by the algorithm team, but co-confirmed by business owners, data owners, and platform teams on objects, relationships, constraints, and evidence.

## 21.3 Information extraction and entity linking

Information extraction converts text, tables, and system records into entities and relations. Traditional NER/RE, rule-based methods, LLM extraction, and VLM page understanding can all contribute, but enterprise systems care more about the verifiability of extraction results. Each fact should ideally include evidence, confidence, source, extractor version, and review status. When comparing extraction approaches in Table 21-5, the emphasis remains on verifiability. Rules, traditional models, LLMs, VLMs, and human review are not mutually exclusive choices, but are combined according to fact risk and document format.

*Table 21-5: Information Extraction Approaches. Source: compiled by the author.*

| Approach           | Advantages                      | Risks                                    |
|--------------------|--------------------------------|------------------------------------------|
| Rules and Dictionaries | Explainable, stable, low cost   | Low coverage, increasing maintenance cost |
| Traditional NER/RE  | Suitable for fixed entity types and bulk text | Requires labeled data, limited cross-domain transfer |
| LLM Extraction     | Quick to start, handles complex semantics | Hallucination, format drift, cost and consistency issues |
| VLM Extraction     | Suitable for invoices, screenshots, page layouts | Low confidence and visual misjudgments require review |
| Human Review       | High quality for high-risk facts | High cost, limited throughput            |

This explains why the later fact JSON must retain `evidence`, `confidence`, `extractor`, and `review_status` fields. Without these, even the most advanced extraction pipeline cannot enter enterprise governance. Entity linking is high-risk for knowledge graph functionality. `Alibaba Cloud`, `Aliyun`, and `Ali Cloud` may represent the same provider; "key account customer" and "strategic customer" may be synonyms in some business lines but not others. Entity linking must combine names, aliases, business keys, source systems, context, and human confirmation. It cannot rely solely on embedding similarity.

Entity linking errors are more dangerous than extraction omissions. Missing one relation causes under-answering by the system; wrongly merging two distinct customers, suppliers, or metrics causes unrelated facts to connect, and GraphRAG may generate seemingly coherent explanations following this incorrect path. Conversely, splitting one entity into multiple nodes affects analysis and risk aggregation by missing high-risk links. Therefore, entity linking should retain candidates, scores, decision basis, and human review status. For high-risk entities, a "candidate first, confirm later" write strategy should be adopted.

```json
{
  "subject": {"type": "Contract", "id": "contract-2026-001"},
  "predicate": "belongs_to",
  "object": {"type": "Customer", "id": "customer-8842"},
  "evidence": {
    "source_id": "contract-2026-001",
    "page": 1,
    "span": "Party A: East China Branch"
  },
  "confidence": 0.91,
  "extractor": "llm-extractor-v2",
  "review_status": "approved"
}
```

The key pathway for entity linking in Figure 21-4 follows the same principle: name similarity is only a candidate source, final confirmation must combine business keys, source systems, contextual evidence, and human review to avoid incorrectly merging same-name customers, products, or similar metrics.

## 21.4 Graph databases and GraphRAG architecture

Graph databases provide relationship storage and query capabilities; Neo4j, NebulaGraph, and similar systems can all serve as the backbone of an enterprise knowledge graph. The core idea of GraphRAG is not to "stuff the graph into a prompt," but to let the graph structure assist with retrieval, aggregation, path explanation, and community summarization. Microsoft GraphRAG extracts documents into a graph, performs community detection and summarization, and then supports global and local search; Neo4j's GraphRAG ecosystem emphasizes combining graph queries with vector retrieval. These two approaches both demonstrate that graphs and vectors are complementary, not competing. GraphRAG retrieval should be decomposed into several distinct modes, as shown in Table 21-6, so that teams can route queries by problem type. Not every question warrants a global search, and not every question should pass through graph queries first.

*Table 21-6: GraphRAG retrieval modes. Source: compiled by the authors.*

| Mode | Approach | Suited for |
|---|---|---|
| Local search | Start from an entity and traverse neighbors, paths, and evidence | Localized relationships for a specific customer, contract, or service |
| Global search | Answer based on community summaries or global themes | Cross-department, cross-document, or trend-level questions |
| Vector + Graph | Vector search identifies candidate entities/documents; the graph then expands relationships | Queries expressed vaguely by the user but pointing to a locatable target entity |
| Graph + Text evidence | Graph paths supply structure; document chunks supply evidence | High-stakes answers and synthesis questions that require citations |

Production systems typically combine these modes: vector search first locates candidate entities, the knowledge graph expands relationships, and text evidence provides citations. High-stakes answers in particular must retain the final text-evidence step instead of presenting only graph paths. The principal risk with GraphRAG is that **a wrong graph is more persuasive than no graph at all**. If an erroneous relationship enters the knowledge graph, the LLM may follow it to produce a structurally coherent but factually incorrect explanation. GraphRAG responses must therefore include facts, evidence, and confidence scores-more than node paths.

A second risk is **structured hallucination**. The system may retrieve a real path yet interpret the relationships along that path as stronger causal connections than they actually are; it may also treat a generalized description from a community summary as a fact about a specific entity. GraphRAG answers should clearly distinguish three categories of content: "a relationship exists in the graph," "the evidence text supports this conclusion," and "the model has inferred this from the relationship." For high-stakes questions, graph paths should serve only as an organizing framework; final conclusions must still be grounded in text evidence, factual confidence scores, and effective timestamps. The GraphRAG pipeline shown in Figure 21-5 consists of several stages: vector recall, graph expansion, text evidence retrieval, and answer generation. Graph paths provide structure; text chunks provide evidence; both must carry access-control and version metadata.

![Figure 21-5: GraphRAG retrieval architecture](../../images/part4/en/ch21-02-graphrag-architecture.svg)

*Figure 21-5: GraphRAG retrieval architecture. Source: original illustration by the authors. Alt text: A query fans out simultaneously to vector retrieval for relevant passages and to graph traversal for related entities; the two result streams are merged before being passed to generation, with arrows illustrating how GraphRAG combines semantic similarity with relational reasoning.*
## 21.5 Knowledge asset governance

After a knowledge graph goes live, the main task is not to continue extraction but to govern it. Entities will merge and split, relationships will expire, contracts will change, metric scopes will adjust, and business terminologies will be renamed. Knowledge asset governance must answer: who owns the ontology, who reviews facts, which relationships can be used by Agents, which facts are expired, and which answers reference those facts. Governance should be broken down into verifiable items as shown in Table 21-7, absorbing all previous content: ontologies must be versioned; facts must have sources; nodes and edges must have permissions; Agent usage of the graph must be traceable.

*Table 21-7: Knowledge Asset Governance Checklist. Source: Compiled by the author.*

| Governance Item | Requirements |
|---|---|
| Ontology Versioning | Entity types, relation types, attributes, and constraints can be versioned |
| Fact Source | Each fact has source, evidence, extractor, and reviewer |
| Permission Boundaries | Graph nodes and edges inherit business system ACL or have separate configuration |
| Lifecycle Management | Creation, update, deprecation, merge, and split have audit trails |
| Quality Evaluation | Extraction accuracy, linking accuracy, conflict rate, and orphan node rate are observable |
| Agent Usage | Which tools, RAG processes, and DataAgent queries use the graph must be traceable |

## 21.6 Ontology changes and semantic-layer coordination

Ontology changes affect downstream extraction, graph queries, permissions, and DataAgent reasoning. When an entity type, relation type, or constraint changes, the platform should assess affected extractors, graph schema, semantic-layer mappings, evaluation samples, and answer templates. Business owners approve semantic meaning; platform owners manage interfaces and rollout; data owners confirm source keys and lineage.

Chapter 33's semantic layer and the knowledge graph should coordinate instead of duplicate definitions. Metrics, dimensions, and table fields usually belong in the semantic layer; entity relationships, contract links, service dependencies, and evidence-backed facts belong in the graph. DataAgent can combine them when a question needs both metric calculation and relationship explanation.

## 21.7 Applicability boundary for GraphRAG

GraphRAG is useful when questions rely on entities, relationships, impact chains, or cross-document synthesis. It is unnecessary for simple policy Q&A where structured chunks and citations already provide reliable answers. It is risky when ontology quality, extraction accuracy, and permission boundaries are immature, because errors in a graph can make generated answers look more coherent than the evidence supports. The production boundary should be explicit. Use GraphRAG for supplier-contract-risk chains, service dependency analysis, incident impact tracing, and metric lineage explanations. Keep ordinary document lookup on RAG until relation modeling clearly reduces errors or review effort.

## 21.8 Extraction quality and human verification

Extraction quality should be measured by entity accuracy, relation accuracy, linking accuracy, orphan node rate, conflict rate, and evidence coverage. Average extraction accuracy is insufficient if high-risk relation types, such as contract liability or customer ownership, remain unreviewed. Facts should carry confidence, evidence, extractor version, and review status. Human verification should focus on high-impact facts, low-confidence relations, entity merges, and ontology changes. Reviewed facts become training and regression samples for extraction. Rejected facts should also be retained as negative samples so future extractors do not repeat the same mistakes.

## 21.9 Permission and audit for knowledge assets

Knowledge graphs introduce permission problems beyond document ACLs. Nodes, edges, evidence snippets, community summaries, graph paths, and derived explanations may each expose sensitive information. A user allowed to read a public supplier profile may still be unauthorized to see litigation risk edges or contract amount evidence.

The platform should evaluate permissions before graph traversal, before evidence expansion, and before answer generation. Audit logs should record the user, query, graph version, traversed entities, returned edges, cited evidence, and masking policy. This allows incident review when a relationship leak occurs through a seemingly harmless graph answer.

## 21.10 Product boundary for knowledge engineering

Knowledge engineering should start with a narrow product boundary. A first release may cover a few entity types, a few relation types, and a small set of high-value questions. It should prove extraction quality, review workflow, permission control, and answer citation before expanding to a full enterprise graph.

The product boundary also prevents premature OWL/RDF-first design. Standards are useful when cross-organization exchange, formal reasoning, or compliance demands them, but many enterprise teams first need stable business keys, evidence, lifecycle, and governance. Once those basics are in place, standard semantic technologies can be introduced deliberately instead of becoming an early modeling burden.

## 21.11 Knowledge graph release and fact rollback

Knowledge graph release is more sensitive than ordinary document indexing. It changes candidate text, entities, relationships, paths, and reasoning entry points at the same time. Adding a relation type may let GraphRAG traverse one more hop. Merging two entities may connect contracts, customers, and risk events that were previously separate. Deprecating an ontology field may remove the context needed to explain historical facts. Graph release records should therefore bind ontology version, extractor version, entity-linking rules, fact batch, and permission policy.

Before release, the platform should run a shadow graph. The shadow graph receives the same candidate facts but does not affect online answers. The platform compares entity hits, path length, relationship evidence, permission trimming, and answer citations between the old and new graph on representative questions. Review should not focus only on how many new edges were added. It should ask whether the new edges have sources, whether they passed review, and whether they changed high-risk answers. If a supplier is wrongly merged with a same-name entity, the new graph may make impact analysis look more complete while connecting unrelated contracts. The shadow graph is where this kind of defect should be caught.

Fact rollback needs fine-grained design. If an extractor upgrade writes a batch of low-confidence relations, the platform should be able to roll back by fact batch. If entity-linking rules merge customers incorrectly, the platform should split the entities and restore affected edges. If a relation direction is wrong in the ontology, the platform should roll back the schema version and rerun affected queries. Rolling back an entire database snapshot is often too coarse because it may remove correct updates made in the same period. A more controllable approach treats rollback as fact state changes, supported by source, batch, extractor, review status, and lifecycle state on each fact.

Graph deletion should also distinguish "stop using in new answers" from "delete historical evidence." Expired contracts, inactive policies, and retired relations should stop participating in new answers, while historical reports and audits may still need to explain why an earlier conclusion was produced. The platform can mark facts as `retired` or `inactive` while preserving versions and evidence. Physical deletion should be reserved for data deletion requests, tenant exits, or retention expiry. This keeps current answers clean without breaking auditability.

A first knowledge-engineering platform does not need a full graph operations console, but it should keep the release and rollback evidence from the beginning: ontology version, fact batch, review status, permission tag, release time, retirement time, and affected question set. Once GraphRAG participates in production answers, these records become the basis for debugging and trust. Without them, the team can only see that an edge exists today; it cannot explain when the edge appeared, who approved it, why it was used, or how to withdraw it.

## 21.12 Knowledge asset operating ledger and feedback path

Once a knowledge graph enters Agent workflows, it needs an operating ledger that records changes to ontology, facts, extractors, and usage scenarios. The ledger should include ontology version, fact batch, extractor version, human review status, permission tags, release time, retirement time, affected question set, and owner. When DataAgent or GraphRAG generates an answer, the platform can then explain which graph version was used, which facts were reviewed, which relations were only candidate evidence, and which facts have been retired. Without this ledger, a larger graph only makes online disputes harder to locate.

The feedback path should connect business users to the knowledge engineering team. When users find incorrect entity merges, expired relations, wrong evidence citations, or graph paths that overstate a conclusion, the feedback should not stay in a support ticket. The platform should locate entity ID, relation ID, evidence chunk, ontology version, and extractor version, then route the item to the owner. The result should update the fact lifecycle: confirmed, corrected, retired, split entity, merged entity, or added as a negative sample. Feedback then changes the graph instead of only correcting one conversation.

Knowledge asset operations should also examine usage. Some relation types may be extracted at high volume but rarely support high-quality answers. Some entity types may be frequently retrieved but often blocked by permission. Some ontology changes may make GraphRAG paths longer without improving evidence hits. The platform should regularly inspect graph usage, answer citation rate, human correction rate, permission denial rate, retired-fact ratio, and isolated-node ratio. These measures help the team decide which ontology areas deserve expansion, which extraction rules should become stricter, and which relation types should stay out of production answers for now.

The first version can keep the ledger lightweight. Each graph release stores version, fact batch, and regression result. Each user feedback item stores affected entities, relations, and review conclusion. Each retired relation keeps historical evidence and stop-use time. With these records in place, the graph can be corrected over time. A fuller knowledge-asset console can come later without losing early fact sources and decisions.

The ledger should also control graph expansion. Before adding a new entity type or relation type, the team should know which questions it serves, who reviews it, and how rollback works when it is wrong. Relations without a clear question and owner should stay in an experimental graph or candidate-fact pool before they enter production GraphRAG. Graph growth then follows business questions and evidence capacity, not the urge to connect every possible relationship.

The operating goal should stay restrained. A larger graph is not automatically better. The facts that matter are the ones used by business questions, supported by evidence, controlled by permissions, and recoverable through rollback.

## 21.13 Version review for knowledge engineering changes

Knowledge engineering changes cover a wide surface: document parsing, chunk policy, metadata rules, terminology, synonyms, permission labels, knowledge catalogs, citation format, and answer templates. Any of these changes can alter RAG and Agent behavior. Version review should record them in the same chain. Otherwise, when users see answer changes, the team can only guess whether the model, retrieval, or knowledge base caused the difference.

Review material should include knowledge domain, change type, sample set, pass rate, failure samples, affected Agents, rollback method, and owner. If terminology changes improve recall, the team should record which queries improved. If chunk policy makes citations more accurate, the team should also check context cost. If permission-label changes reduce leakage risk, the team should verify recall was not weakened too much. Knowledge quality should be measured by whether answers become more verifiable and reviewable, not by document count alone.

Version review should also support multiple languages. Chinese knowledge, English knowledge, and translated material may use different tokenization, terminology, and citation forms. If Chinese and English knowledge bases evolve separately, cross-language Agent answers can cite inconsistent evidence. The platform should record material correspondence, translation state, citation priority, and update time. Bilingual book drafts, bilingual product docs, and cross-border business scenarios can then follow one knowledge-governance standard.

## 21.14 Joint maintenance of knowledge engineering and business processes

After knowledge engineering goes live, maintenance cannot sit only with a graph or document team. Business-process changes alter entities, relationships, rules, and evidence priority. When contract approval changes, clause risk, approval roles, and effective conditions may change. When a product line changes, product entities, version relationships, and customer applicability may change. If knowledge assets do not follow the process, GraphRAG and Agent reasoning can cite outdated relationships while the answer still looks structured and factual.

Joint maintenance should embed knowledge changes into business change processes. When a policy is added, a product changes, an organization is adjusted, or a new metric goes live, the business owner should decide whether ontology, entity linking, rule extraction, permission labels, or evaluation samples are affected. The platform team moves the change through the knowledge release process. The knowledge engineering team extracts and verifies. The business owner confirms semantics. This keeps knowledge engineering from becoming a one-time modeling project and makes it part of business-system evolution.

A first version can cover only core entities and high-risk relationships. Each knowledge change should record source, affected scope, validation samples, and rollback method. Each user report about wrong knowledge should return to the related entity or relationship instead of stopping at an answer template. The value of knowledge engineering appears when enterprise facts can be maintained and explained over time, not when the first graph looks complex.

## 21.15 Business acceptance after knowledge graph changes

Knowledge graph changes need business acceptance after the build finishes. A construction report can show entity counts, relation counts, and extraction accuracy, but business risk often sits in specific paths. A supplier merged into the wrong entity, a contract clause connected to the wrong product line, or an inverse metric dependency can make GraphRAG produce a complete but wrong explanation. Business acceptance should inspect these paths instead of relying on aggregate graph size.

Acceptance samples should come from real business questions: which products are affected by this supplier failure, which contract templates are affected by this policy, or which downstream reports are affected by this metric anomaly. Each sample should inspect entity hits, relation path, source evidence, permission trimming, and final answer. A correct graph path without supporting text should fail. Supporting text without permission labels should also fail for production answers. GraphRAG acceptance must evaluate structure and evidence together.

Business acceptance should produce a state for each finding. Accepted relations enter the production graph. Low-confidence but useful relations enter a candidate graph. Disputed relations enter manual review. Wrong relations become negative samples. This gives graph changes more states than release or reject. Each state has a purpose: production graph for formal answers, candidate graph for recall support, and negative samples for preventing repeated extraction errors.

A first version can use a fixed sampling workflow. After each graph release, sample high-risk entities, frequent relation types, recent changes, and online feedback cases. Business owners and the knowledge-engineering team review them together. The result writes back to fact lifecycle and evaluation samples. Knowledge engineering then carries real business responsibility instead of delivering a graph that only looks complete.

## 21.16 Joint acceptance across knowledge engineering and retrieval

After knowledge engineering goes live, it should be accepted together with RAG, the semantic layer, and the DataAgent chain. If the team inspects only the graph, entities and relations may look correct. If it inspects only retrieval, relevant documents may be reachable. Real user questions usually cross several layers: the semantic layer identifies metrics and dimensions, retrieval finds policies or reports, the knowledge graph adds entity relations and impact paths, and the generation model writes the explanation. A version change in any layer can shift the final answer.

Joint acceptance should start from real tasks. A supplier-risk question should inspect supplier entity, related products, contract clauses, risk events, source documents, permission trimming, and final answer. A metric-anomaly question should inspect metric definition, downstream reports, business entities, GraphRAG path, and DataAgent query evidence. Acceptance material should not stop at "graph hit" or "document hit." It should show whether the evidence set supports the conclusion. A correct graph path with stale text evidence should fail. A correct document with a wrong entity link should also fail.

Joint acceptance also needs conflict handling. A customer dimension in the semantic layer may use a different definition from the customer entity in the graph. A policy effective date in a RAG document may conflict with fact lifecycle in the graph. An abnormal metric returned by DataAgent may not match the impact path proposed by GraphRAG. The platform should record these conflicts as acceptance samples and ask the business owner to decide which definition applies, whether more evidence is required, and whether a relation should be held out of production answers. Leaving the conflict to the generation model makes the answer read smoothly while the evidence base remains unstable.

A first platform version can maintain a small cross-chain sample set. Each sample contains user question, semantic-layer fields, retrieval evidence, graph path, permission context, expected answer, and failure handling. When the graph, ontology, index, or semantic layer changes, the platform replays these samples. Knowledge engineering then becomes part of DataAgent evidence, connected to Chapter 17 retrieval quality, Chapter 33 semantic layer, Chapter 38 Trace, and Chapter 39 Eval.

## 21.17 Knowledge asset retirement and impact communication

Knowledge engineering teams often focus on adding entities, relations, and rules, while retirement receives less attention. Enterprise knowledge assets expire when organizations change, products are discontinued, policies are retired, metric definitions change, or contract templates are replaced. If old facts remain in the graph or index, GraphRAG may still find a complete path, and users may not notice that the path no longer supports a current answer. Retirement governance treats knowledge assets as production objects with lifecycles, not as material that stays valid after one ingestion.

Retirement needs impact analysis before removal. The platform should identify retrieval samples, graph paths, Agent tool descriptions, semantic-layer fields, and report templates affected by the retired entity, relation, rule, or document. If a fact is used only by internal debugging samples, it can be marked as historical. If it supports official policy Q&A or approval recommendations, the business owner should confirm the replacement interpretation and transition period. Deleting without impact analysis can create a different failure: old facts disappear, the system starts refusing answers, and users do not know which new fact applies.

Retirement is not the same as physical deletion. The production graph can remove a current relation while the audit graph keeps the historical state, source evidence, effective period, and retirement reason. RAG should also distinguish current material, historical material, and retired material. If users ask what policy applies now, the system should prefer current material. If they ask why a decision was made last year, historical material may still matter. Permission and retention rules determine how long historical knowledge remains available, who can access it, and whether it can enter model context.

Impact communication should reach product and operating materials. After a knowledge asset is retired, the related Agent capability description, FAQ, data-product documentation, and evaluation samples should change as well. For frequent questions, the interface can temporarily tell users that the old interpretation has been retired and point to the new policy version. This is more reliable than forcing users to infer meaning from changed answers. The knowledge-engineering team should also add retirement samples to regression tests so that old relations do not reappear during a later extraction or import.

## 21.18 Change approval for knowledge assets

After knowledge engineering reaches production, a successful demo is not enough evidence. The platform needs stable fields for fact source, entity relation, version diff, impact scope, approver, and rollback path, and those fields should connect to release records, Trace, evaluation samples, and incident notes. When a production issue appears, teams can follow one set of facts to understand scope, ownership, and repair order instead of stitching together model logs, business logs, and verbal explanations.

This evidence also connects the surrounding chapters. It links to Chapter 18 on vector stores, Chapter 20 on RAG, and Chapter 33 on the semantic layer: upstream capabilities provide assumptions, downstream capabilities consume the result, and governance capabilities preserve evidence and review decisions. If these materials do not share identifiers and versions, the production system splits apart. Business owners see user complaints, platform owners see system errors, and security or compliance teams see explanations written after the fact. That separation makes it hard to decide whether the issue came from data, model behavior, tool contracts, workflow state, or organizational ownership.

Common production risks include wrong facts being reused by several Agents, relation changes not being communicated downstream, and old graphs still being served from cache. These risks are less visible during demos because demos usually exercise the successful path. Production users bring boundary cases, repeated requests, permission changes, and long-running state. The platform team should turn such failures into release samples. Some samples should block launch, some can be handled by degradation, and some require the business owner to accept the remaining risk with a review date.

Knowledge-asset changes should be managed like data-model changes, with source, approval, and impact records. The record can stay compact, but it should include time, version, owner, sample, action, and the next review condition. Without those fields, review remains informal experience. With them, one production issue can become material for later releases, evaluation suites, and training.

A first platform version can start with a small set of high-risk paths. Choose flows with high traffic, high business impact, or sensitive data, require an evidence package for each change, and then expand the practice to ordinary scenarios. This keeps the capability at the engineering level: runnable, explainable, and recoverable.
## Chapter Recap

Knowledge engineering makes enterprise business semantics explicit from implicit text and system fields. RAG can find document evidence, knowledge graphs can organize entity relationships, and GraphRAG combines both to support complex Q&A and impact analysis. The challenges usually lie not in graph database syntax, but in ontology design, extraction, entity linking, evidence, permissions, and lifecycle governance.

- Knowledge engineering is a business semantic asset, with RAG, graph paths, and review records maintained together.
- Ontology design should start from business questions, then choose graph structures that can preserve evidence and ownership.
- LLM extraction must include evidence, confidence, version, and review status.
- GraphRAG should return both graph paths and textual evidence to avoid structured hallucinations.

- [ ] Are entity types, relation types, attributes, and constraints defined?
- [ ] Does each fact have evidence, source, extractor version, and review status?
- [ ] Does entity linking combine business keys, aliases, context, and human review?
- [ ] Do graph nodes and edges have permission and lifecycle management?
- [ ] Does GraphRAG output include cited evidence, more than graph paths?
